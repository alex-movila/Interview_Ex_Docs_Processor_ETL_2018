Born in Portsmouth, Dickens left school to work in a factory when his father was incarcerated in a debtors' prison.
Despite his lack of formal education, he edited a weekly journal for 20 years, wrote 15 novels, five novellas, hundreds of short stories
and non-fiction articles, lectured and performed readings extensively, was an indefatigable letter writer, and campaigned vigorously
for children's rights, education, and other social reforms.

Dickens's literary success began with the 1836 serial publication of The Pickwick Tokens.
Within a few years he had become an international literary celebrity,
famous for his humour, satire, and keen observation of character and society.
His novels, most published in monthly or weekly instalments, pioneered the serial publication of narrative fiction, which became
the dominant Victorian mode for novel publication.
